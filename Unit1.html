1.1. Describe the purpose of IT in modern business.

Using information technology (IT) in a modern business enables companies to gain a competitive advantage over other market players through making their businesses more cost-effective by reducing their operating costs, such as transaction and implementation costs, as well as better integrated by making communication and collaboration between all team members of the same team or different teams based in multiple geographic locations more efficient. Integrating various stakeholders of the project together and because of that, facilitating a more efficient data processing, e.g. by using the Enterprise Resource Planning (ERP) and allowing a review of sales, growth, and other operating metrics in real time, IT makes the entire decision-making process quicker, rational, and well grounded. Additionally, more accurate and sophisticated market intelligence techniques used make business processes more cost-effective by adapting their leading strategy to a constantly changing situation on the market and their customers’ needs. Identifying new customer preferences and developing and applying innovative customer requirements into the already existing business processes, results then in to a greater customer satisfaction and revenue stream due to reaching a wider customer pool in a shorter period of time.

1.4. Describe the risks that might impact digital projects – what causes digital projects to fail? Why?

There are several risks, which may have a detrimental impact on digital projects and lead to their failure, such as:
1. Delays caused by either a wrong methodology or IT system used.
2. Higher costs of a project caused by a low accuracy and/ or poor time management, resulting in the project’s delay.
3. Built non-functional end product due to a poor communication among all developers and other stakeholders involved in the project.
4. Built non-satisfactory end product due to a low technical skills of the programme developers involve in the project.

1.5. Describe how you would go about selecting and using IT systems and software – how do you select the right kind of technology for the job?

Selecting and deciding on the right kind of IT software and systems for a digital project requires several steps to be followed, such as:
1. To identify and list all IT software and systems suitable for a project’s main objectives and capable to solve existing problems.
2. To budget and decide what price are we willing to pay for IT software.
3. To identify the software requirements to meet, such as functional, usability, technological preferences, budgetary, reporting, scalability, and vendor requirements.
4. To rank the requirements (listed above) as fundamental and optional.
5. To exclude unsuitable IT software/ solutions, in terms of technology preferences and budget available.
6. To evaluate and assess a short-listed (max 5-10) IT software/ solutions against the software’s usability criteria, e.g. by using a requirements matrix in a spread sheet.
7. To assess and compare learnability, efficiency, memorability, errors, and satisfaction of the first-time users of the trialled IT software/ solutions.
8. To do due-diligence of the selected software, in terms of the vendor requirements.
9. To negotiate business terms, such as implementation time, support levels, service availability, and payment terms with the chosen software provider.
10. To finalise a deal and sign a business contract.[1]

1.7. Describe legal guidelines and constraints that impact digital projects – How does GDPR impact digital projects?

The legal environment around a digital project development varies depending on the geographic location of the web development companies themselves and their chosen customer base. For instance, software developers based in the European Union (EU) and serving the customers in the EU come under the GDPR, which is the EU’s new data protection law and apparently, the toughest privacy and security law in the world. [5, 6] However, companies based outside the EU (e.g. in the USA) but serving the EU customers, may be also the subject to the provision of the GDPR and face heavy penalties if not using and securing their customers’ personal data appropriately. [6, 7] The only exemption to this rule is purely personal and household activity, or professional and commercial activity by the small and medium-size companies with less than 250 employees. [6]
